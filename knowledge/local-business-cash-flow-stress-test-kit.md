# Local Business Cash Flow Stress-Test Kit

## See What Breaks Before It Breaks You

A slow week is manageable—until the truck breaks, weather closes the doors, customers pay late, and payroll keeps marching toward Friday like it has somewhere important to be.

This practical workbook helps a local business model those shocks before they become emergency decisions. Use it to build a 13-week cash-flow baseline, test individual and stacked disruptions, identify response triggers, prepare funding documents, and create a one-page summary for a lender, accountant, advisor, or internal team.

> **Important:** This resource is for planning and education. It does not guarantee funding approval and does not replace accounting, legal, tax, insurance, lending, or underwriting judgment. Verify all calculations and protect sensitive financial information.

# How to Use This Kit

Complete the sections in order:

1. Enter the current business position.
2. Build the 13-week baseline.
3. Run the slow-week scenario.
4. Run the broken-equipment scenario.
5. Run the weather-closure scenario.
6. Combine disruptions in the stacked-shock calculator.
7. Identify the seven critical stress-test numbers.
8. Assign green, yellow, and red response triggers.
9. Organize the funding document packet.
10. Produce the one-page lender or advisor summary.

## Recommended Weekly Routine

Run this review every Monday:

- Replace the previous week’s estimates with actual results.
- Update available cash.
- Update receivables and expected payment dates.
- Add new expenses and obligations.
- Rerun the scenarios.
- Review trigger levels.
- Assign next actions and owners.

A forecast that never gets updated is just financial fan fiction.

## Funding Gap Formula

**Funding Gap = Maximum of $0 or (Protected Minimum Cash − Lowest Projected Cash)**

## Planning Buffer

**Illustrative Planning Buffer = Funding Gap × 10% to 20%**

A buffer is not permission to borrow blindly. It is an allowance for uncertain costs, delayed recovery, or estimates that turn out to be suspiciously optimistic.

- Which number surprised you most?
- Which expense caused the most pressure?
- Which receivable matters most?

- Which action creates new risk?
